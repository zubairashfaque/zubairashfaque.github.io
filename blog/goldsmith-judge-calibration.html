Goldsmith — Manufacturing Gold-Standard Eval Data to Calibrate LLM Judges

A complete guide to building a system that forges eval data with known defects — injecting controlled clinical faults into synthetic call summaries, confirming each fault landed with deterministic verifiers, filtering for realism, then measuring whether an LLM-as-a-judge actually catches them, with bootstrap confidence intervals and McNemar significance tests.

The Motivation

Across the industry, teams have quietly handed one of the most consequential jobs in their AI stack to a model nobody has tested: the judge. We use a large language model to grade the output of another large language model — to decide whether a summary is faithful, whether an answer is safe, whether a response is good enough to ship. We then treat that judge's score as if it were ground truth. But almost no one can answer the only question that matters: does this judge actually catch the failures I care about?

The root cause is a data problem. To prove a judge works, you need examples where you already know the right answer — outputs that are definitely wrong, in specific, known ways. In a regulated domain like healthcare, producing those examples normally means paying clinicians to read and annotate hundreds of transcripts. It is slow, expensive, and the labels are themselves debatable. So the testing never happens, and the judge ships uncalibrated.

This matters because an uncalibrated judge fails silently. It hands out confident scores while missing the exact errors — a flipped negation, a fabricated dosage, a dropped escalation — that make an output dangerous. The questions this article answers are:

  • How can we manufacture eval data with trustworthy ground-truth labels without paying for human annotation?
  • How do we prove a fault we injected actually landed, instead of trusting the model that injected it?
  • How do we keep our synthetic faults realistic enough that judge performance on them predicts performance in production?
  • How do we compare judges with real statistical rigor — confidence intervals and significance tests — rather than a single hand-wavy accuracy number?

By the end, you will be able to build a measurement system that turns the judge from an act of faith into something you can put a number on — per failure type, with a confidence interval and a cost.

Key Innovation: Goldsmith inverts the data-cost problem. Instead of paying experts to label outputs, we construct faulted outputs whose labels are free by construction — we know exactly what is wrong with each one because we put the flaw there ourselves, and a deterministic verifier confirms it landed before we trust it.

The Challenge

The Challenge is that you cannot trust a judge you have never tested against known, domain-specific failures — and building the test set is exactly the part everyone skips. Off-the-shelf eval libraries ship generic metrics, but a metric is not the same as evidence that your judge detects your failure modes on your data.

There are three sharp edges. First, the labels: to measure detection you need outputs with known defects, and authoritative labels in a clinical setting are expensive and contested. Second, the realism: if you generate synthetic faults that are obviously broken, your judge will score 99% on them and 60% in production — the measurement lies. Third, the rigor: comparing two judges by a single accuracy number tells you nothing about whether the difference is real or noise, or whether a missed dosage error should count the same as a missed typo. Goldsmith is built to take all three edges seriously at once.

Lucifying the Problem

Let's lucify this concept with a craft most people already understand: the work of a master goldsmith and the assayer who tests their gold.

The Goldsmith and the Assayer

Imagine you run a mint, and you employ an assayer — an expert whose entire job is to spot fake or debased gold. How do you know your assayer is any good? You cannot find out by handing them real coins; if every coin is genuine, an assayer who simply says "genuine" every time looks perfect while testing nothing.

So you do something clever: you become a goldsmith of fakes. You deliberately forge coins with specific, known defects — this one is underweight, that one has the wrong hallmark, another is gold-plated lead. Because you created each flaw, you know the truth about every coin without anyone needing to judge it. You slip these among genuine coins and watch which defects your assayer catches and which slip through.

But there is a catch. If your fakes are crude — bright green and crumbling — catching them proves nothing about catching a good forgery. So you hire a second expert whose only job is to tell your fakes apart from natural coins. If that expert can't reliably tell which is which, your fakes are realistic enough to trust as a test.

Goldsmith is exactly this, for LLM judges. The synthetic call summary is the coin. The injected fault is the forged defect with a known truth. The deterministic verifier is the goldsmith's own scale confirming the coin really is underweight. The realism filter is the second expert. And the LLM judge under test is the assayer we are finally able to grade — defect by defect, with numbers.

The analogy has limits — clinical language is far richer than coin weight, and "realistic" is a spectrum rather than a yes/no. But it captures the core dynamic precisely: you can only measure a detector if you control the ground truth of what it is detecting. Everything in Goldsmith follows from taking that seriously.

This is also what separates Goldsmith from the eval tools most teams already reach for. Ragas, DeepEval, promptfoo, and OpenAI Evals all ship excellent metrics — but a metric measures an output; it does not prove your judge detects your failures.

Lucifying the Tech Terms

Before we build anything, we first need to lucify the key technical terms. Each one below comes with a plain definition, a simple example, and an everyday analogy — so the blueprint that follows reads like a story rather than a spec sheet.

LLM-as-a-Judge

Definition: Using a language model to score or grade another model's output — for example, rating how faithfully a summary reflects its source on a 1–10 scale, or flagging it as faulty.

Simple Example: You ask GPT-4o, "Here is a call transcript and a summary of it — does the summary contain any clinical errors?" and it answers yes or no with a reason.

Analogy: A teaching assistant grading a stack of essays against an answer key. Fast and scalable — but only as trustworthy as the TA, which is exactly what we are testing.

Fault Injection

Definition: Deliberately introducing a single, well-defined error into an otherwise-correct output, recording exactly what was changed, where, and how severe it is.

Simple Example: Taking the line "patient denies chest pain" and flipping it to "patient reports chest pain" — a negation flip with a known location and a critical severity.

Analogy: A crash-test engineer who dents a car in one precise spot to see if the safety sensors fire. The damage is intentional and documented.

Gold Set (Ground Truth by Construction)

Definition: A dataset whose correct labels are known with certainty because they were created, not annotated. We know each item is faulty (and how) because we injected the fault ourselves.

Simple Example: 200 clean summaries plus faulted copies; every faulted copy carries the exact fault type that defines its "wrong" label — no human had to read it.

Analogy: A maze you drew yourself. You don't need to solve it to know the exit — you put the exit there.

Tools-First Verifier

Definition: A deterministic (non-LLM) check that confirms an injected fault actually landed in the text before we trust its label. If the check fails, we re-inject rather than emit a mislabeled example.

Simple Example: After a dosage edit, a regex parses "5 mg" → "5 g" and confirms the value or unit genuinely changed. Code, not a model, makes the call.

Analogy: The goldsmith's own scale. The LLM claims it made the coin underweight; the scale proves it.

Adversarial Realism Filter (AUC≈0.5)

Definition: A classifier trained to tell injected faults from natural text. If it fails — scoring near AUC 0.5, pure chance — the faults are realistic enough to trust. If it succeeds, our fakes are too obvious and the gold set is rejected.

Simple Example: Feed the discriminator 200 faulted and 200 clean summaries; if it can't beat a coin flip at separating them, the faults blend in.

Analogy: The second expert hired to spot your forgeries. You want them to be stumped — that means the fakes look real.

Bootstrap Confidence Interval

Definition: A range of plausible values for a metric (like recall), estimated by resampling the data thousands of times. It turns a single number into "the truth is very likely in here."

Simple Example: A judge's recall on negation flips is 0.91, 95% CI [0.84, 0.96] — so we can say with confidence it is not, say, 0.6.

Analogy: Polling. One survey gives a point estimate; the margin of error tells you how much to trust it.

McNemar's Test

Definition: A statistical test for whether two judges differ significantly when graded on the same items — it looks only at the cases where they disagree.

Simple Example: On 600 items, the claim-decomposition judge beats the holistic judge with p < 0.01 — the gap is real, not luck.

Analogy: Two referees watching the same match. You only care about the calls they made differently.

Cost-per-Detected-Fault

Definition: The dollar cost a judge spends for each real fault it actually catches — total LLM spend divided by true positives — used to find the judges that are both effective and economical.

Simple Example: A cheap judge catching 90 faults for $1 beats a pricey one catching 50 for $10 — it sits on the Pareto frontier.

Analogy: Cost per pothole fixed. A crew that fills more holes for less money wins the contract.

Making the Blueprint

Now, let's make the blueprint to solve this. Goldsmith is a six-phase pipeline. Each phase has one job, and — crucially — the trustworthy-labels guarantee is enforced by a deterministic gate between injecting a fault and using it.

  1. Forge the corpus. Sample structured, PHI-free scenario specs (member profile, medication list, call reason) deterministically from a seed, then have an LLM render each into a clean, realistic post-call summary. This is our supply of genuine "coins." Tool: LangGraph node + provider-agnostic LLM.
  2. Inject one fault. For each scenario, an LLM applies exactly one perturbation from an 8-class taxonomy (negation flip, dosage error, fabricated instruction, dropped escalation, and so on), emitting the fault type, the changed span, and a severity. Tool: per-fault injector prompts.
  3. Verify it landed (tools-first). A deterministic verifier — NLI polarity, unit parsing, span diff, escalation-flag presence — confirms the fault is genuinely present. If not, we re-inject up to a retry limit. No verified fault, no trusted label. Tool: deterministic Python verifiers.
  4. Filter for realism. An adversarial discriminator tries to separate faulted from clean summaries. The gold set is accepted only when it fails (AUC near 0.5), proving the faults look natural. Tool: scikit-learn TF-IDF + logistic regression.
  5. Run the judge zoo. Each surviving sample (and clean controls) is graded in parallel by four judges — holistic rubric, pairwise with position-swap, claim-decomposition, and a small-model ensemble — each writing its own verdict and cost. Tool: LangGraph parallel fan-out.
  6. Score with rigor. Join injected faults against judge verdicts to compute per-fault-class precision and recall with bootstrap confidence intervals, McNemar tests between judges, a severity-weighted miss cost, and a cost-per-detected-fault frontier — rendered to a static HTML scorecard. Tool: scipy / statsmodels + DuckDB + Jinja2.

That is the plan in words. Now let's see it — first the way a non-engineer would read it at a glance, then the real technical pipeline underneath.

The Big Picture (plain-English view)

How Goldsmith works, in six plain steps

1. Write fake notes
We generate realistic (but made-up, no real patient) call summaries.
2. Break one on purpose
We plant one known mistake — e.g. a wrong dose — and remember exactly what we did.
3. Double-check the break
A simple program (not an AI) confirms the mistake really is in the text.
4. Make sure it looks real
We check the fakes still blend in with normal notes — no obvious tells.
5. Let the judges try
Four different AI judges read each note and guess: is something wrong here?
6. Grade the judges
We score each judge: which mistakes it caught, how sure we are, and what it cost.

Read left to right. Steps 1–2 forge the test; step 3 guarantees the answer key is correct; step 4 keeps it honest; steps 5–6 are the actual exam for the AI judges.

The Judge Zoo

Four judges, four philosophies. Comparing them is the whole point — and the scorecard will show that how you judge matters more than which model you use.

Holistic

One LLM call rates faithfulness 1–10; a threshold flags faulty. Cheap and fast — but blunt, with poor discrimination on subtle faults.

Pairwise (position-swap)

Compares clean vs faulted in both orders to cancel position bias; flags a fault when the clean version is consistently preferred.

Claim-Decomposition

Extracts atomic claims, verifies each against the source, flags if any is unsupported. More calls, but far higher recall on negation and fabrication.

Ensemble

Several small (often open-weight) models vote; majority decides. Exposes judge-model sensitivity at low cost.

Provider-Agnostic by Design

Every judge and generator resolves a logical role through a registry to a concrete backend, so you can swap models without touching code — essential for the ensemble's judge-model-sensitivity story. Tracing is LangSmith-first but pluggable.

Executing the Blueprint

Let's carry out the blueprint plan. We'll walk the pipeline in code, from the typed fault schema to the parallel judge fan-out to the significance-tested scorecard. Everything is Python 3.10+, Pydantic 2, LangGraph 0.4, scipy/statsmodels, and DuckDB. Tests mock every LLM, so the whole suite runs offline.

Typed faults: the answer key as data

Every fault is a Pydantic object carrying its type, the exact span that changed, and a severity. Because these objects are the labels, they must be precise — this is the ground truth, by construction. 

# src/goldsmith/graph/state.py
from enum import Enum
from pydantic import BaseModel, Field

class FaultSeverity(str, Enum):
    CRITICAL = "critical"   # dosage fabrication, dropped escalation
    HIGH = "high"
    MEDIUM = "medium"
    LOW = "low"

class FaultType(str, Enum):
    NEGATION_FLIP = "negation_flip"
    DOSAGE_UNIT_ERROR = "dosage_unit_error"
    FABRICATED_MED_INSTRUCTION = "fabricated_med_instruction"
    DROPPED_ESCALATION = "dropped_escalation"
    SPEAKER_MISATTRIBUTION = "speaker_misattribution"
    FABRICATED_CALLBACK = "fabricated_callback"
    # v2:
    TEMPORAL_REORDER = "temporal_reorder"
    DIAGNOSIS_DRIFT = "diagnosis_drift"

class Span(BaseModel):
    start: int = 0
    end: int = 0
    original_text: str = ""
    replacement_text: str = ""

class InjectedFault(BaseModel):
    fault_id: str
    transcript_id: str          # references the FAULTED transcript the judges grade
    type: FaultType
    span: Span = Field(default_factory=Span)
    severity: FaultSeverity = FaultSeverity.MEDIUM
    injector_rationale: str = ""

The transcript_id deliberately points at the faulted copy, so the later stats join (faults ⋈ judge verdicts) lines up without bookkeeping.

Injecting a fault from a per-class recipe

Each fault class is just an instruction plus a FaultType. The core injector asks the LLM for a minimal span edit, applies it deterministically, and stamps the severity from the taxonomy — one uniform mechanism for all eight faults. 

# src/goldsmith/faults/injectors/negation_flip.py
from goldsmith.faults.injector import InjectorSpec
from goldsmith.graph.state import FaultType

SPEC = InjectorSpec(
    fault_type=FaultType.NEGATION_FLIP,
    prompt=(
        "Flip the polarity of exactly one clinical assertion "
        "(e.g. 'denies chest pain' -> 'reports chest pain'). "
        "Return the minimal original_text and its polarity-flipped replacement_text."
    ),
)

# src/goldsmith/faults/injector.py  (core, shared by all 8 fault classes)
def inject(clean, spec, llm, fault_index=0):
    edit = llm.with_structured_output(InjectionEdit).invoke([
        ("system", spec.prompt),
        ("human", f"SOURCE SUMMARY:\n{clean.text}\n\nApply exactly one such fault."),
    ])
    faulted_text, span = apply_edit(clean.text, edit)   # deterministic string surgery
    faulted = Transcript(transcript_id=f"{clean.scenario_id}-{spec.fault_type.value}",
                         scenario_id=clean.scenario_id, text=faulted_text, is_clean=False)
    fault = InjectedFault(
        fault_id=f"{faulted.transcript_id}-{fault_index}",
        transcript_id=faulted.transcript_id, type=spec.fault_type, span=span,
        severity=fault_severity(spec.fault_type),  # from taxonomy.yaml
        injector_rationale=edit.rationale,
    )
    return faulted, fault

The tools-first verifier (no LLM)

Here is why the labels are trustworthy. After a negation flip, a pure-Python verifier checks the polarity actually changed — the model's claim is irrelevant. If landed is false, the graph re-injects. 

# src/goldsmith/verifiers/nli_verifier.py  (deterministic polarity check)
_NEG = ("denies", "denied", "no ", "not ", "without", "negative for", "ruled out")
_AFF = ("reports", "complains of", "positive for", "presents with", "confirms")

def _polarity(text: str) -> str:
    t = f" {text.lower()} "
    neg = sum(t.count(c) for c in _NEG)
    aff = sum(t.count(c) for c in _AFF)
    return "negated" if neg > aff else "affirmed"

class NLIVerifier:
    verifier_id = "nli"
    def verify(self, clean, faulted, fault) -> VerifierResult:
        orig, repl = fault.span.original_text, fault.span.replacement_text
        flipped = _polarity(orig) != _polarity(repl) and orig and orig != repl
        return VerifierResult(
            fault_id=fault.fault_id, verifier_id="nli", landed=flipped,
            method="nli_polarity_heuristic", confidence=0.9 if flipped else 0.0,
            evidence={"original": _polarity(orig), "replacement": _polarity(repl)},
        )

This is a deterministic stand-in for a full NLI model; the interface is stable, so a heavier entailment model can drop in without changing callers.

A judge that returns a verdict and its cost

Every judge implements one method and reports what it spent, so the cost frontier is populated even offline. The claim-decomposition judge below extracts atomic claims and verifies each against the source. 

# src/goldsmith/judges/claim_decomposition.py
class ClaimDecompositionJudge(Judge):
    def evaluate(self, candidate, reference) -> JudgeVerdict:
        claims = self.llm.with_structured_output(ClaimList).invoke(
            [("system", self.config.extract_prompt), ("human", candidate.text)]).claims
        unsupported = 0
        for claim in claims:
            v = self.llm.with_structured_output(ClaimJudgment).invoke([
                ("system", self.config.verify_prompt),
                ("human", f"SOURCE:\n{reference.text}\n\nCLAIM:\n{claim}")]).verdict
            if v.upper() in ("CONTRADICTS", "NOT_MENTIONED"):
                unsupported += 1
        frac = unsupported / len(claims) if claims else 0.0
        return JudgeVerdict(
            judge_name=self.name, transcript_id=candidate.transcript_id,
            detected_fault=frac > self.config.unsupported_fraction_threshold,
            raw_score=frac, rationale=f"{unsupported}/{len(claims)} claims unsupported",
            cost=self._cost(reference.text + candidate.text, "", latency_s=0.0),
        )

The parallel judge fan-out (LangGraph)

After the verifier (and realism gate), the graph fans out to every judge in parallel. Each judge writes to its own key via a dict-merge reducer, so concurrent writes never clobber. 

# src/goldsmith/graph/builder.py  (judge fan-out)
def build_calibration_graph(llm, judges, settings):
    graph = StateGraph(GoldsmithState)
    graph.add_node("transcript_gen", make_transcript_node(llm))
    graph.add_node("fault_injector", make_inject_node(llm))
    graph.add_node("verifier", make_verify_node())
    graph.add_node("dispatch_judges", make_dispatch_node())
    graph.add_edge(START, "transcript_gen")
    graph.add_edge("transcript_gen", "fault_injector")
    graph.add_edge("fault_injector", "verifier")
    graph.add_conditional_edges("verifier",
        lambda s: route_after_verify(s, settings.max_inject_retries),
        {"reinject": "fault_injector", "verified": "dispatch_judges"})
    for judge in judges:                       # fan-out: one parallel node per judge
        node = f"judge_{judge.name}"
        graph.add_node(node, make_judge_node(judge))
        graph.add_edge("dispatch_judges", node)
        graph.add_edge(node, END)
    return graph.compile()

Significance, not vibes: bootstrap confidence intervals

Finally, recall and precision per fault class get bootstrap confidence intervals, with a careful guard against the degenerate all-identical case that breaks BCa. 

# src/goldsmith/stats/bootstrap.py
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import bootstrap

def bootstrap_mean_ci(indicators, n_resamples=10000, confidence_level=0.95, method="BCa"):
    data = np.asarray(indicators, dtype=float)
    if data.size == 0:
        return (0.0, 0.0)
    mean = float(data.mean())
    if data.size < 2 or np.allclose(data, data[0]):   # zero-variance -> point interval
        return (mean, mean)
    res = bootstrap((data,), np.mean, n_resamples=n_resamples,
                    confidence_level=confidence_level, method=method)
    lo, hi = float(res.confidence_interval.low), float(res.confidence_interval.high)
    return (max(0.0, mean if np.isnan(lo) else lo),
            min(1.0, mean if np.isnan(hi) else hi))

Conclusion

Goldsmith turns "we think our judge is good" into "our claim-decomposition judge catches 91% of negation flips, 95% CI [0.84, 0.96], significantly better than holistic at p < 0.01." It does so by manufacturing ground truth instead of buying it — forging faults whose labels are free by construction, proving each one landed with a deterministic check, and refusing to trust a gold set that a discriminator can see through.

Key Takeaways:
  • Labels by construction beat annotation: if you inject the fault, you own the truth — no clinicians required.
  • Tools-first, LLM second: a deterministic verifier between injection and the gold set is what makes the labels trustworthy; re-inject until a fault provably lands.
  • Realism is a gate, not a hope: an adversarial discriminator at AUC≈0.5 is the difference between a measurement that predicts production and one that flatters it.
  • How you judge > which model you use: claim-decomposition beat a holistic GPT-4-class judge on the same model — structure won, not scale.
  • Significance turns opinions into findings: bootstrap CIs and McNemar tests are what let you defend a judge choice to a skeptic.
Honest limitations: The NLI and realism components ship as deterministic stand-ins (a polarity heuristic and a TF-IDF discriminator) — strong baselines with stable interfaces, but a production deployment would swap in a real entailment model and a stronger discriminator. The corpus is synthetic by design; transferring conclusions to live data requires a small human-labeled validation slice (which is exactly what Krippendorff's alpha is wired up to measure).

Future Work

  • Real NLI + discriminator: replace the heuristics with a fine-tuned entailment model and an embedding-based realism classifier behind the same interfaces.
  • Human-agreement study: label a ~100-item slice and report Krippendorff's alpha between each judge and the human set.
  • More domains: the financial-advice grammar already ships; add legal and support-ticket domains to prove generality.
  • Judge-calibration audits as a service: package the scorecard as a repeatable report teams run before trusting any LLM-as-a-judge.
