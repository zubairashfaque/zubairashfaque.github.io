MultiGuard: Multimodal Hate Detection — From CLIP+RoBERTa to Production Cascaded Inference

Transform pre-trained vision and language models into a multimodal classifier using 3 fusion strategies, knowledge distillation, and cascaded inference — from research to Docker-served API.

March 12, 2026 35 min read Zubair Ashfaque
Multimodal AI CLIP RoBERTa Fusion Knowledge Distillation GradCAM FastAPI

The Motivation

Memes combine text and images to create meaning that neither modality has alone. A photo of a puppy is harmless. The text "they should all go back" is ambiguous. But overlay that text on a photo of refugees — the combined message is hateful. Social media platforms moderate billions of such posts daily, and unimodal AI (text-only or image-only) systematically misses this cross-modal hate.

Detecting multimodal hate speech is one of the hardest open problems in content moderation. Pre-trained models like CLIP understand images and RoBERTa understands text — but neither understands the relationship between the two. MultiGuard bridges that gap with a complete pipeline: from pre-trained backbones through three fusion strategies, knowledge distillation for compression, GradCAM explainability, and cascaded inference for production serving.

The questions this blog answers:

  • How do you detect hate that only emerges when text and image are combined?
  • How do three different fusion strategies (late, cross-attention, tensor) compare in capturing cross-modal interactions?
  • How do you compress a ~310M parameter teacher into an ~87M student without losing accuracy?
  • How do you explain why the model flagged a meme — which image region and which text tokens drove the decision?
  • How do you deploy at scale with cascaded inference, where ~70% of requests are handled in ~5ms?
  • What does the complete pipeline look like from Facebook Hateful Memes dataset to Docker-served API?
Key Innovation: A 3-stage training pipeline (baseline late fusion → fusion ablation with cross-attention → knowledge distillation) trained locally on NVIDIA Quadro T2000 (3.62 GB VRAM). The distilled student achieves best AUROC (0.6452) and accuracy (0.598) while remaining lightweight at 853K params. Cascaded inference routes ~70% of clear cases through CLIP zero-shot (~5ms), with the full model handling ambiguous cases.

The Challenge

The Challenge is building a system that understands the combined meaning of text and images, not just each modality in isolation — and doing so in a way that is explainable, compressible, and deployable at production scale.

Five interconnected problems make this hard:

  1. Pre-trained models understand modalities separately. CLIP was trained on 400M image-text pairs for contrastive matching — not for classification. RoBERTa was trained on 160GB of text — with zero visual understanding. Neither can classify hateful memes out of the box.
  2. The dataset contains confounders. Facebook's Hateful Memes (~10K memes) was specifically designed with confounders — benign confounders that look hateful by one modality but are safe, and hateful confounders that look safe by one modality but are hateful. This defeats naive approaches.
  3. Fusion is an open research question. How do you combine 512-dim vision features with 768-dim text features? Concatenation? Attention? Outer products? Each captures different types of cross-modal interaction, and the optimal choice is not obvious.
  4. Production demands compression. A ~310M parameter teacher with CLIP ViT-B/16 + RoBERTa is too expensive for real-time serving. Knowledge distillation to EfficientNet-B0 + DistilRoBERTa achieves 2.6x compression and 4.3x faster inference while retaining 95% of teacher AUROC.
  5. Decisions must be explainable. Content moderation requires transparency. GradCAM shows which image regions triggered the decision; Integrated Gradients shows which text tokens mattered. Without explainability, the system is a black box.

Lucifying the Problem

Let's lucify this concept with two analogies that make the architecture intuitive.

Analogy 1: Two Detectives

Imagine two expert detectives investigating a crime. Detective Vision can see the crime scene perfectly but cannot hear anything — no audio from the security camera. Detective Language can hear all the audio recordings but is blindfolded — cannot see a single photo. Each detective writes a brilliant report from their single modality. But neither can solve a case where the crime is only visible when you combine what was said with what was shown.

That is exactly the multimodal hate detection problem. CLIP is Detective Vision — trained on 400 million image-text pairs, it can "see" images with superhuman precision. RoBERTa is Detective Language — trained on 160GB of text, it understands language nuance. But a meme that combines an innocent photo with an ambiguous caption requires both detectives to share their notes. Fusion is that collaboration.

Analogy 2: Court Translators

Think of two world-class translators who have never worked in a courtroom. One translates Arabic perfectly, the other translates Mandarin perfectly. But put them in a complex international trial and they fail — not because they cannot translate, but because they do not know courtroom procedure. Lucifying the Tech Terms

Before we build, let's define every technical term you'll encounter. Each term gets a Definition, a Simple Example, and an Analogy.

1. CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training)

Definition: A vision model trained by OpenAI on 400 million image-text pairs using contrastive learning. Given an image, CLIP produces a 512-dimensional embedding vector that captures visual semantics. MultiGuard uses ViT-B/16 — a Vision Transformer with 16x16 patches.

Simple Example: Feed a photo of a cat → CLIP outputs a 512-number vector. Feed a photo of a dog → CLIP outputs a different 512-number vector. These vectors are close to the text "a photo of a cat" and "a photo of a dog" respectively.

Analogy: CLIP is a museum curator who has seen 400 million artworks with their descriptions. Show any new artwork and the curator can instantly describe its content — but cannot judge whether it violates gallery policy.

2. RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT Approach)

Definition: A transformer language model trained on 160GB of text (books, Wikipedia, news). It has 125M parameters and produces 768-dimensional embeddings from the [CLS] token — a single vector summarizing the entire input text, up to 77 tokens.

Simple Example: Feed "This is a funny meme" → RoBERTa outputs a 768-number vector. Feed "This is a hateful meme" → different 768-number vector. The difference encodes semantic meaning.

Analogy: RoBERTa is a literature professor who has read every book in the library. They understand sarcasm, irony, and nuance — but have never seen a picture in their life.

3. Late Fusion

Definition: The simplest fusion strategy. Concatenate vision features [512] and text features [768] into a single [1280] vector, then pass through an MLP: Linear(1280→512) + GELU + Dropout → Linear(512→256) + GELU + Dropout. No cross-modal interaction — features are combined but never "talk" to each other.

Simple Example: Vision says [0.8, 0.2, ...] and text says [0.1, 0.9, ...]. Late fusion concatenates them into [0.8, 0.2, ..., 0.1, 0.9, ...] and feeds the combined vector through neural network layers.

Analogy: Two experts write separate reports. A manager reads both reports side-by-side and makes a decision. The experts never discuss with each other.

4. Cross-Attention Fusion

Definition: A deep fusion strategy where each modality attends to the other. Vision-to-text: 4 TransformerDecoderLayers where vision queries attend to text keys/values. Text-to-vision: 4 TransformerDecoderLayers in the opposite direction. Results are combined via a learned gate: gate = Sigmoid(Linear(concat(v2t, t2v))), fused = gate * v2t + (1-gate) * t2v. Uses 8 attention heads per layer.

Simple Example: The vision feature "asks" the text feature: "What does the text say about what I'm seeing?" Simultaneously, the text feature asks: "What does the image show about what I'm reading?" Both answers are combined via a learned gate.

Analogy: Two detectives interviewing each other about the case. Each asks probing questions about what the other observed, and a judge weighs both interrogation reports to reach a verdict.

5. Tensor Fusion (Low-Rank)

Definition: A multiplicative fusion strategy. Project both modalities to a low-rank space: vision_factor = Linear(512→32) and text_factor = Linear(768→32). Compute element-wise product: fused = v_factor * t_factor. Then project back: Linear(32→768) + Dropout + LayerNorm. Rank=32 approximates the full outer product while keeping computation tractable.

Simple Example: Vision feature compressed to 32 numbers, text feature compressed to 32 numbers. Multiply corresponding elements: vision[i] × text[i] for all i. The product captures how each visual concept interacts with each textual concept.

Analogy: Mixing paint — red and blue separately are just colors, but when you mix them you get purple, a fundamentally new color. The multiplication creates meaning that neither input had alone.

6. Knowledge Distillation

Definition: Model compression technique. A large teacher model (CLIP ViT-B/16 + RoBERTa + best fusion, ~310M params) teaches a small student (EfficientNet-B0 + DistilRoBERTa + late fusion, ~87M params). The student learns from both hard labels (ground truth) and soft labels (teacher's probability distribution at temperature T=4.0). Loss: alpha * CE(student, labels) + (1-alpha) * T² * KL(student_soft, teacher_soft), alpha=0.5.

Simple Example: Teacher says "85% hateful, 15% safe" (soft). Ground truth says "hateful" (hard). Student learns from both signals — the soft target teaches that this case is "clearly hateful" rather than "borderline."

Analogy: A master chef teaching an apprentice. Instead of just giving recipes (hard labels), the master explains why each ingredient works — the reasoning (soft probabilities) transfers understanding, not just answers.

7. Focal Loss

Definition: A modified cross-entropy loss that down-weights easy examples and focuses on hard ones. Formula: FL = alpha * (1-pt)^gamma * CE, where alpha=0.25 and gamma=2.0. When the model is confident (pt near 1.0), the loss is near zero. When the model is wrong (pt near 0.0), the loss is maximal.

Simple Example: If the model is 95% confident and correct, focal loss is almost zero — it moves on. If the model is 40% confident on a hard example, the loss is high — it focuses training on that case.

Analogy: A teacher who spends extra time on students struggling with a concept, instead of re-explaining what everyone already understands.

8. AUROC (Area Under the ROC Curve)

Definition: A threshold-independent metric that measures how well the model separates positive and negative classes across all possible thresholds. 0.5 = random guessing, 1.0 = perfect separation. Plots True Positive Rate vs False Positive Rate.

Simple Example: AUROC of 0.85 means: pick a random hateful meme and a random safe meme — 85% of the time, the model assigns a higher probability to the hateful one.

Analogy: A metal detector's overall sensitivity. AUROC doesn't tell you the specific threshold setting — it tells you how good the detector is across all possible sensitivity settings.

9. F1 Score

Definition: The harmonic mean of precision and recall: F1 = 2 * (precision * recall) / (precision + recall). Precision = "of all flagged content, what fraction is actually hateful?" Recall = "of all hateful content, what fraction did we catch?" F1 balances both.

Simple Example: A filter with 90% precision but 50% recall catches half the hate but rarely makes mistakes. F1 = 0.64 — the harmonic mean penalizes the low recall.

Analogy: A security guard's effectiveness. Precision is "does the guard stop the right people?" Recall is "does the guard catch all the threats?" F1 balances both.

10. Gradient Accumulation

Definition: Simulate large batch training on limited GPU memory. Process small batches (e.g., 16), accumulate gradients for N steps (e.g., 4), then update weights once. Effective batch size = batch_size × accumulation_steps = 64.

Simple Example: GPU can only fit 16 samples. Set gradient_accumulation_steps=4. After 4 forward passes (64 samples total), one optimizer step runs. Same training dynamics as batch_size=64.

Analogy: Collecting opinions from four small focus groups before making a decision, instead of surveying all 64 people at once in a room that doesn't fit them.

11. Cosine Annealing with Warmup

Definition: A learning rate schedule. Start at zero, linearly ramp up during warmup (warmup_ratio=0.1 means first 10% of training). Then follow a cosine curve from peak LR down to zero. Prevents early instability and enables fine convergence.

Simple Example: With 10 epochs and warmup_ratio=0.1: epoch 1 = warmup (LR ramps from 0 to 1e-4). Epochs 2-10 = cosine decay (LR smoothly decreases from 1e-4 toward 0).

Analogy: Warming up before exercise, then gradually cooling down. You don't sprint immediately (that pulls a muscle), and you don't stop abruptly (that causes cramps).

12. Mixed Precision (FP16)

Definition: Use 16-bit floating point for forward/backward passes instead of 32-bit. FP16 uses 1 sign bit + 5 exponent bits + 10 mantissa bits. Half the memory of FP32 with higher mantissa precision than BF16 (but narrower exponent range). Weights stored in FP32, computations in FP16 with loss scaling to prevent underflow. Used on Tesla P100 which does not support BF16.

Simple Example: FP32: 4 bytes per number. FP16: 2 bytes per number. A 310M parameter model uses roughly half the memory. Training is ~2x faster.

Analogy: Writing shorthand notes instead of full sentences. You lose some detail (precision) but capture the same range of ideas (exponent range), and you can write twice as fast.

13. GradCAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping)

Definition: An explainability technique that produces a heatmap showing which image regions contributed to a prediction. Hooks capture activations and gradients from a target layer. Formula: CAM = ReLU(sum(w_c * A_c)) where weights w_c = global average pooling of gradients, A_c = activations.

Simple Example: Model predicts "hateful" for a meme. GradCAM highlights the faces in the image — showing the model focused on the people depicted, not the background.

Analogy: A forensic investigator dusting for fingerprints. GradCAM reveals which parts of the image the model "touched" most heavily when making its decision.

14. ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange)

Definition: A framework-agnostic model format. Export a PyTorch model to ONNX, then run it with ONNX Runtime — which applies graph optimizations (operator fusion, constant folding) for faster inference without any code changes.

Simple Example: Train in PyTorch → export to .onnx → serve with ONNX Runtime. Inference is 2-3x faster than native PyTorch because the runtime optimizes the computation graph.

Analogy: A universal adapter that lets any appliance (model) plug into any outlet (serving framework). The adapter also includes a voltage optimizer (graph optimizations).

Making the Blueprint

Now, let's make the blueprint to solve this. The MultiGuard pipeline has 8 stages, from raw data to production deployment.

  1. Data Pipeline: Facebook Hateful Memes (~10K memes), image preprocessing (224x224), text tokenization (77 tokens), augmentation (crop, flip, jitter, synonym replacement, mixup/cutmix).
  2. Stage 1 — Baseline (Late Fusion): Freeze CLIP + RoBERTa backbones, train a concat+MLP fusion head. Establishes baseline performance with the simplest architecture.
  3. Stage 2a — Cross-Attention Fusion: 4-layer bidirectional cross-attention with gated combination. Vision attends to text, text attends to vision. Deepest cross-modal interaction.
  4. Stage 2b — Tensor Fusion: Low-rank (rank=32) element-wise product in projected space. Multiplicative interaction captures joint meaning that neither modality has alone.
  5. Stage 3 — Knowledge Distillation: Best fusion model becomes teacher. Train a 2.6x smaller student (EfficientNet-B0 + DistilRoBERTa) using soft+hard targets (T=4.0, alpha=0.5).
  6. Explainability: GradCAM for image regions, Integrated Gradients for text tokens, SHAP for global feature importance. Every prediction can be explained.
  7. Serving: FastAPI + cascaded inference (CLIP zero-shot fast path at ~5ms, full model for ambiguous cases at ~50ms) + Redis caching + Docker containerization.
  8. Monitoring: Evidently drift detection, automated retraining triggers (performance drop >5%, drift >0.1), MLflow experiment tracking, Airflow orchestration.

Late Fusion Implementation

@FUSION_REGISTRY.register("late_fusion")
class LateFusion(nn.Module):
    """Late fusion via concatenation and MLP.
    Simplest fusion strategy — serves as baseline."""

    def __init__(
        self,
        vision_dim: int = 512,
        text_dim: int = 768,
        hidden_dims: list[int] | None = None,
        dropout: float = 0.1,
    ) -> None:
        super().__init__()
        hidden_dims = hidden_dims or [512, 256]
        input_dim = vision_dim + text_dim

        layers: list[nn.Module] = []
        for dim in hidden_dims:
            layers.extend([
                nn.Linear(input_dim, dim),
                nn.GELU(),
                nn.Dropout(dropout),
            ])
            input_dim = dim

        self.mlp = nn.Sequential(*layers)
        self.output_dim = hidden_dims[-1]

    def forward(
        self, vision_features: torch.Tensor, text_features: torch.Tensor
    ) -> torch.Tensor:
        combined = torch.cat([vision_features, text_features], dim=-1)
        return self.mlp(combined)
Analogy: Two experts write separate reports. A manager reads both reports side-by-side and makes a decision. The experts never discuss with each other — the MLP is the manager, the concatenation is the side-by-side reading.

Step 3: Stage 2a — Cross-Attention Fusion

Cross-attention is the deepest fusion strategy. Instead of simply concatenating features, each modality attends to the other through 4 transformer decoder layers with 8 heads each. Vision queries attend to text keys/values (V2T path), and text queries attend to vision keys/values (T2V path). The two paths are combined via a learned gate.

Cross-Attention Implementation

@FUSION_REGISTRY.register("cross_attention")
class CrossAttentionFusion(nn.Module):
    """Bidirectional cross-attention fusion.
    Vision attends to language and language attends to vision,
    then results are combined via learned gating."""

    def __init__(
        self,
        hidden_dim: int = 768, num_heads: int = 8,
        num_layers: int = 4, feedforward_dim: int = 2048,
        dropout: float = 0.1,
    ) -> None:
        super().__init__()
        self.output_dim = hidden_dim
        self.v2t_layers = nn.ModuleList([
            nn.TransformerDecoderLayer(
                d_model=hidden_dim, nhead=num_heads,
                dim_feedforward=feedforward_dim,
                dropout=dropout, batch_first=True,
            ) for _ in range(num_layers)
        ])
        self.t2v_layers = nn.ModuleList([
            nn.TransformerDecoderLayer(
                d_model=hidden_dim, nhead=num_heads,
                dim_feedforward=feedforward_dim,
                dropout=dropout, batch_first=True,
            ) for _ in range(num_layers)
        ])
        self.gate = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim * 2, hidden_dim), nn.Sigmoid(),
        )

    def forward(self, vision_features, text_features):
        v = vision_features.unsqueeze(1)  # [B, 1, D]
        t = text_features.unsqueeze(1)    # [B, 1, D]
        v2t = v
        for layer in self.v2t_layers:
            v2t = layer(v2t, t)
        t2v = t
        for layer in self.t2v_layers:
            t2v = layer(t2v, v)
        v2t, t2v = v2t.squeeze(1), t2v.squeeze(1)
        gate_weight = self.gate(torch.cat([v2t, t2v], dim=-1))
        return gate_weight * v2t + (1 - gate_weight) * t2v
Analogy: Two detectives interviewing each other about the case. Each asks probing questions about what the other observed. The learned gate is the judge who decides how much weight to give each detective's cross-examination.

Step 4: Stage 2b — Tensor Fusion

Tensor fusion captures multiplicative interactions between modalities. Instead of concatenation (additive) or attention (query-key), it computes the element-wise product in a low-rank projected space. This creates features that represent the joint meaning — something neither modality has alone.

Tensor Fusion Implementation

@FUSION_REGISTRY.register("tensor_fusion")
class TensorFusion(nn.Module):
    """Low-rank tensor fusion network.
    Computes outer product in low-rank factorized form."""

    def __init__(
        self, vision_dim: int = 512, text_dim: int = 768,
        hidden_dim: int = 768, rank: int = 32, dropout: float = 0.1,
    ) -> None:
        super().__init__()
        self.output_dim = hidden_dim
        self.vision_factor = nn.Linear(vision_dim, rank, bias=False)
        self.text_factor = nn.Linear(text_dim, rank, bias=False)
        self.fusion_weights = nn.Linear(rank, hidden_dim)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        self.layer_norm = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_dim)

    def forward(self, vision_features, text_features):
        v_factor = self.vision_factor(vision_features)  # [B, rank]
        t_factor = self.text_factor(text_features)       # [B, rank]
        fused = v_factor * t_factor                      # [B, rank]
        fused = self.fusion_weights(fused)               # [B, hidden_dim]
        fused = self.dropout(fused)
        return self.layer_norm(fused)
Analogy: Mixing paint — red and blue separately are just colors, but when you mix them you get purple, a fundamentally new color. The element-wise multiplication creates joint features that represent the interaction between vision and language, not just their sum.

Step 5: Stage 3 — Knowledge Distillation

The best fusion model (cross-attention, 310.8M parameters) is too expensive for production real-time serving. Knowledge distillation compresses it into a student model (86.7M parameters) — 2.6x smaller and 4.3x faster — while retaining 95% of teacher AUROC. The student uses lightweight backbones: EfficientNet-B0 (vision) and DistilRoBERTa (text) with late fusion.

Distillation Loss Implementation

class DistillationTrainer(MultimodalTrainer):
    """Trainer for knowledge distillation.
    Uses hard label loss + soft KL-divergence loss."""

    def __init__(
        self, teacher_model: nn.Module,
        temperature: float = 4.0, alpha: float = 0.5, **kwargs
    ) -> None:
        super().__init__(**kwargs)
        self.teacher_model = teacher_model
        self.teacher_model.eval()
        for param in self.teacher_model.parameters():
            param.requires_grad = False
        self.temperature = temperature
        self.alpha = alpha

    def compute_distillation_loss(
        self, student_logits, teacher_logits, labels
    ) -> torch.Tensor:
        hard_loss = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
        soft_student = F.log_softmax(
            student_logits / self.temperature, dim=-1
        )
        soft_teacher = F.softmax(
            teacher_logits / self.temperature, dim=-1
        )
        soft_loss = F.kl_div(
            soft_student, soft_teacher, reduction="batchmean"
        )
        soft_loss = soft_loss * (self.temperature ** 2)
        return self.alpha * hard_loss + (1 - self.alpha) * soft_loss

Training Techniques Deep Dive

Eight techniques work together to make MultiGuard training stable, memory-efficient, and effective on imbalanced data.

1. Focal Loss

FL = α · (1 - pt)γ · CE   |   α=0.25, γ=2.0
class FocalLoss(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, alpha: float = 0.25, gamma: float = 2.0):
        super().__init__()
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.gamma = gamma

    def forward(self, logits, targets):
        ce_loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, targets, reduction="none")
        pt = torch.exp(-ce_loss)
        focal_loss = self.alpha * (1 - pt) ** self.gamma * ce_loss
        return focal_loss.mean()

Analogy: A teacher who spends extra time on students struggling with a concept, instead of re-explaining what everyone already understands. The (1-pt)^γ term makes the loss near-zero for easy examples.

Analogy: A speed limit on the highway. You can go in any direction, but the maximum speed is capped. If gradients "speed" past 1.0, they are scaled back.

7. AdamW Optimizer

weight_decay=0.01 — decoupled weight decay regularization. Each parameter gets its own adaptive learning rate based on first and second moment estimates. Weight decay is applied directly to weights, not through gradients.

Analogy: A GPS with separate speed and route controls. Adam adapts the "speed" (learning rate) per parameter, while weight decay acts as "friction" that prevents any single parameter from growing too large.

8. InfoNCE Contrastive Loss

class InfoNCELoss(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, temperature: float = 0.07):
        super().__init__()
        self.temperature = temperature

    def forward(self, embeddings_a, embeddings_b):
        logits = torch.matmul(embeddings_a, embeddings_b.T) / self.temperature
        labels = torch.arange(logits.shape[0], device=logits.device)
        loss_a = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)
        loss_b = F.cross_entropy(logits.T, labels)
        return (loss_a + loss_b) / 2

Analogy: A matching game — given a batch of N image-text pairs, the model must match each image to its correct text among N-1 distractors. Temperature=0.07 controls how "sharp" the matching distribution is.

GradCAM Implementation

class GradCAM:
    """Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping."""

    def __init__(self, model: nn.Module, target_layer: nn.Module):
        self.model = model
        self.target_layer = target_layer
        self.gradients = None
        self.activations = None
        self._register_hooks()

    def _register_hooks(self):
        def forward_hook(module, input, output):
            self.activations = output.detach()
        def backward_hook(module, grad_input, grad_output):
            self.gradients = grad_output[0].detach()
        self.target_layer.register_forward_hook(forward_hook)
        self.target_layer.register_full_backward_hook(backward_hook)

    def generate(self, input_tensor, target_class=None):
        self.model.eval()
        output = self.model(input_tensor)
        if target_class is None:
            target_class = output.argmax(dim=-1).item()
        self.model.zero_grad()
        output[0, target_class].backward()
        weights = self.gradients.mean(dim=[2, 3], keepdim=True)
        cam = (weights * self.activations).sum(dim=1, keepdim=True)
        cam = torch.relu(cam).squeeze()
        cam = (cam - cam.min()) / (cam.max() - cam.min() + 1e-8)
        return cam
How GradCAM works: Hook-based gradient capture. During the forward pass, activations A are captured at the target layer. During backward pass, gradients dL/dA are captured. The heatmap is: CAM = ReLU(Σ wc · Ac), where weights wc are the global average pooled gradients. The result shows which spatial regions in the image contributed most to the prediction.

Cascaded Inference

Not all content needs the full fusion model. MultiGuard uses a two-stage cascade: Stage 1 runs CLIP zero-shot classification (~5ms). If CLIP's confidence exceeds 0.85, the prediction is returned immediately. If confidence is below 0.85, Stage 2 runs the full fusion model (~50ms). In practice, ~70% of requests are handled by the fast path.

Conclusion

MultiGuard demonstrates a complete pipeline for multimodal hate detection — from pre-trained CLIP ViT-B/16 and RoBERTa backbones through three fusion strategies, knowledge distillation for 2.6x compression (with 4.3x faster inference), GradCAM explainability, and cascaded inference for production serving. Trained locally on NVIDIA Quadro T2000 (3.62 GB VRAM) with FP16 mixed precision.

The key architectural decisions:

  • 3-stage training: Baseline → Fusion Ablation → Distillation. Each stage builds on the previous, with the best fusion model becoming the teacher for distillation.
  • 3 fusion strategies: Late (concat+MLP), Cross-Attention (bidirectional + gating), and Tensor (low-rank product). Each captures different types of cross-modal interaction.
  • Cascaded inference: CLIP zero-shot handles ~70% of clear cases in ~5ms. Only ambiguous cases go through the full fusion model. Effective throughput scales with content difficulty.
  • Explainability-first: GradCAM for image regions, Integrated Gradients for text tokens, SHAP for global importance. Every prediction can be explained and audited.
  • Production-ready: FastAPI + Docker + Redis + Prometheus/Grafana + Evidently drift detection + Airflow orchestration. The system monitors itself and triggers retraining when performance degrades.
