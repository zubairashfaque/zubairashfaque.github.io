The Motivation

Modern LLMs like ChatGPT and Claude go through a sophisticated post-training pipeline before they become useful: supervised fine-tuning on instructions, alignment through human preferences, retrieval-augmented generation for grounding, and optimization for deployment. This pipeline — pioneered by InstructGPT and refined by Llama-3 — is the engine behind every AI assistant you use today.

The problem? Most tutorials teach you one step. A blog post on LoRA fine-tuning. A video on RAG pipelines. A notebook on GGUF quantization. You learn fragments but never see how the pieces connect into a working system. You certainly never build the entire pipeline yourself, on hardware you actually have.

What if you could build the full InstructGPT-shape pipeline — data curation, supervised fine-tuning, preference alignment, retrieval augmentation, quantization, and deployment — for a medical domain, using only free GPUs and a consumer laptop? That is not a hypothetical. That is what MedAlign-RLHF delivers.

Why medical? Because medicine is the domain where every design choice earns its keep:

  • SFT visibly helps — a general LLM struggles with USMLE questions; a fine-tuned one passes them.
  • Alignment matters — a wrong dosage suggestion is dangerous, not just inconvenient.
  • RAG is necessary — no 7B model can memorize every drug interaction and clinical guideline.
  • Evaluation is well-defined — PubMedQA and MedQA-USMLE provide standardized benchmarks.

The questions this blog answers:

  • How do you fine-tune a 7B model on a free 16 GB GPU?
  • How does DPO teach a model which answers are safer without a reward model?
  • How do you combine dense and sparse retrieval for medical queries?
  • How does domain-specific quantization preserve drug names while shrinking the model to 4.4 GB?
  • How do you prove each pipeline stage actually improved the model?
  • How do you run all of this with zero paid compute?

The Challenge

The Challenge is that building a medical LLM that is both accurate and safe requires solving six interconnected problems simultaneously. Solving any one in isolation produces a system that fails in practice.

1. Teaching Medicine to a General LLM
Mistral-7B knows some medicine from pre-training, but not enough to pass USMLE questions. Full fine-tuning needs ~80 GB VRAM. You have 16 GB.
2. Preference, Not Just Imitation
SFT teaches the model to imitate answers. It does not teach it to prefer safe answers over plausible-but-dangerous ones. Full RLHF (PPO + reward model) needs 4x the memory.
3. Hallucinated Medical Facts
A fine-tuned model still invents drug names and dosages. Pure semantic search misses exact jargon. Pure keyword search misses paraphrases. You need both.
4. Shrinking 14 GB to 4 GB
Naive quantization treats "metformin" and "football" weights equally. Medical accuracy lives in specific weight matrices that get destroyed first.
5. Proving Each Step Helped
"My model is better" means nothing without numbers. You need monotonic improvement on standardized benchmarks: Base < SFT < DPO < DPO+RAG.
6. Zero-Budget Infrastructure
Training needs GPUs (Kaggle). Embedding 211k passages needs GPU. Quantization and serving need CPU. Artifacts must flow between environments for free.

Lucify the Problem

The Medical Resident Analogy

The entire MedAlign pipeline is like the process a hospital uses to train a medical resident — from a smart general-science graduate into a board-certified physician who works in a real clinic.

The Resident's Journey

The Graduate (Base Mistral-7B): Imagine a bright university graduate who speaks English fluently and can follow instructions. They know some medicine from reading Wikipedia, but you would not trust them with a patient. That is our base model — good at following instructions, vague on medical specifics.

The Textbook (Data Preparation): The hospital's curriculum committee gathers 20,178 case studies from two medical schools. Before binding the textbook, they notice many cases are near-copies — "A 23-year-old presents with chest pain..." appears dozens of times with minor wording changes. They run a plagiarism detector (MinHash LSH) and remove 2,032 near-duplicates, keeping 18,146 unique cases. No point making the resident memorize the same case five times.

Medical School (SFT QLoRA): The resident reads all 18,146 case studies twice (2 epochs). But we do not rewrite their entire brain — that would require an operating room the size of a warehouse (80 GB VRAM). Instead, we give them a small notebook (LoRA adapters, 41M parameters, 0.58% of their total knowledge) where they write medical-specific notes. Everything else they already know stays intact. To fit in a small classroom (16 GB T4), their general knowledge is compressed using shorthand (4-bit NF4).

Mock Exams (DPO Pair Generation): After medical school, the hospital runs a mock exam. For each question, the resident writes two answers: one careful and conservative (T=0.3, the textbook answer) and one more creative but risky (T=0.9). An attending physician (GPT-4o-mini) reads both and marks which one they would trust with a real patient. Result: 2,000 graded comparison pairs.

Mentoring (DPO Alignment): The resident sits with the 2,000 graded pairs and internalizes the attending's preferences. "When I was conservative, I was usually right. When I improvised, I sometimes got the dosage wrong." The learning is gentle (40x lower learning rate than medical school) because we do not want to overwrite what they learned — just nudge their judgment. The clever part: normally you need two residents in the room (a policy and a reference). The PEFT-DPO trick uses one resident who toggles between "notebook open" (policy) and "notebook closed" (reference). Saves a whole extra person (14 GB of VRAM).

The Reference Library (RAG Index): The hospital builds a library of 211,000 PubMed abstracts, organized two ways — by topic (FAISS dense embeddings, like a catalog by subject) and by keyword (BM25, like an alphabetical index at the back of a textbook). When the resident gets a question, they check both systems, merge the results (RRF), then have a senior librarian (cross-encoder reranker) re-read the top 20 papers and pick the best 5.

Packing for the Rural Clinic (GGUF Quantization): Graduation day. The resident's full knowledge (14 GB) is too large for a rural clinic with limited resources. They compress it into a field manual (GGUF). But which knowledge to keep at full precision? An importance exam (imatrix) is administered — but tested specifically on drugs, anatomy, and dosages. Weights that fire on "metformin" and "myocardial infarction" keep full precision; weights that fire on "football scores" get compressed more aggressively. Result: a 4.4 GB manual that fits in a backpack.

Board Exams (Benchmarking): The resident takes certification exams at four stages — before school, after school, after mentoring, and with the library open. We expect monotonic improvement: each stage should score higher than the last.

Opening the Clinic (Serving): FastAPI is the back-office phone line (other systems send JSON queries). Gradio is the walk-in reception desk (patients type questions into a form). Both doors lead to the same doctor (shared answer_fn). Docker wraps the clinic into a portable shipping container.

Where the Analogy Breaks Down:
  • Real medical residents learn from patients over years. Our "resident" trains for 3-6 hours per stage.
  • Real preference learning involves thousands of cases over a career. Ours uses 2,000 preference pairs.
  • A real hospital library is curated by domain experts. Ours is automatically indexed from PubMedQA synthetic passages.
  • A real resident integrates learning across their whole career. DPO is a one-shot adjustment — no ongoing learning at inference time.

Lucify the Jargon

Before we dive into the architecture, let's lucify the eight key technical concepts you will encounter throughout this pipeline. Each term gets a formal definition, a concrete example, and a real-world analogy.

1. QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation)

Definition: A technique that freezes a large model in 4-bit precision (saving ~75% memory) and trains only small "adapter" matrices on top. Combines quantization (Q) with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA).

Simple Example: Mistral-7B has 7.28 billion parameters. Full fine-tuning updates all of them and needs ~80 GB VRAM. QLoRA freezes them in 4-bit (~4 GB) and trains only 41.9 million adapter parameters (0.58%) — fitting on a 16 GB GPU.

Analogy: You have a 1,000-page encyclopedia. Full fine-tuning means reprinting the entire encyclopedia with corrections — expensive, and you need a massive printing press. QLoRA means writing corrections on sticky notes placed on top of a compressed microfilm version of the encyclopedia. The sticky notes are small, cheap to produce, and you can swap them out for different topics.

2. DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)

Definition: An alignment method that teaches a model to prefer one answer over another using paired examples, without needing a separate reward model or reinforcement learning loop. Mathematically equivalent to RLHF with KL constraints (Rafailov et al. 2023).

Simple Example: Given a medical question, the model generates two answers. A judge picks the better one. DPO trains the model to make "better" answers more likely and "worse" answers less likely, relative to a frozen reference copy of itself. Our setup: β=0.1 (KL pressure), lr=5e-6, 1 epoch, ~2,000 preference pairs.

Analogy: A student submits two essay drafts to a teacher. The teacher marks "Draft A is better" — no score, no rubric, just a comparison. The student does not need a grading system (reward model) or repeated rounds of feedback (PPO). DPO is the mathematical shortcut that directly adjusts the student's writing style from the comparison alone.

3. PEFT-DPO Memory Trick (ref_model=None)

Definition: A technique that eliminates the need for two full model copies during DPO training by toggling LoRA adapters on and off. Adapters on = "policy" (student learning). Adapters off = "reference" (frozen baseline). One model, two roles.

Simple Example: Normal DPO loads 2 copies of a 7B model = 28 GB VRAM. Won't fit on a T4. PEFT-DPO loads 1 copy, toggles adapters = 14 GB saved. In TRL: set ref_model=None and it handles the toggling automatically.

Analogy: A chess player normally needs two practice partners — one to play against (policy) and one to observe as a baseline (reference). The PEFT trick: one partner plays with their strategy notebook open (adapters on), then closes the notebook (adapters off) to serve as the baseline. Same person, two roles, half the chairs needed.

4. MinHash LSH (Locality-Sensitive Hashing)

Definition: A probabilistic algorithm for finding near-duplicate documents in sublinear time. It hashes documents into compact "signatures" that collide with probability proportional to their Jaccard similarity.

Simple Example: We start with 20,178 medical Q&A pairs. Many are near-copies ("A 23-year-old presents..." repeated with minor wording changes). MinHash LSH at threshold 0.85 identifies and removes ~2,032 near-duplicates in seconds, keeping 18,146 unique samples.

Analogy: You have 20,000 recipes. Some are nearly identical (same dish, different author). Reading every pair to check would take 200 million comparisons. MinHash is like creating a 10-ingredient "fingerprint" for each recipe. Recipes with 8+ matching fingerprint ingredients (85% overlap) are flagged as duplicates. You only need to read the flagged pairs, not all 200 million.

5. Hybrid RAG (Dense + Sparse + RRF + Reranker)

Definition: A retrieval strategy combining semantic search (dense embeddings via BGE-large), keyword search (BM25), Reciprocal Rank Fusion for merging results, and a cross-encoder reranker for fine-grained relevance scoring.

Simple Example: Query: "What are the contraindications of metformin?" Dense retrieval finds passages about "diabetes medication side effects" (meaning match). BM25 finds passages containing the exact word "metformin" (keyword match). RRF merges both ranked lists without score calibration. Cross-encoder reads the top 20 (query, passage) pairs jointly and picks the 5 most relevant.

Analogy: You are searching for a book in a huge library. Librarian A (dense/BGE) understands what you mean even if you use different words, but sometimes misses books with exact technical terms. Librarian B (sparse/BM25) only matches exact words but never misses a specific drug name. You combine their suggestions (RRF = take turns picking from each list). Then a senior librarian (cross-encoder) quickly reads the top 20 book summaries alongside your question and picks the best 5.

6. GGUF Quantization with imatrix

Definition: GGUF is a single-file format for storing and running LLMs (weights + tokenizer + chat template baked in). imatrix (importance matrix) is a calibration step that records which weights are most active for a specific domain, so the quantizer allocates more bits to important weights and fewer to irrelevant ones.

Simple Example: A 14 GB fp16 model is quantized to Q4_K_M (4.4 GB). Without imatrix, accuracy drops ~5%. With imatrix calibrated on 4,000 medical text chunks, accuracy drops only ~2% because drug-name weights keep their precision.

Analogy: You are packing for a trip and can only bring a carry-on bag (4.4 GB). Without guidance (no imatrix), you pack randomly and forget your medication. With a packing list tailored to a medical conference (medical imatrix), you prioritize your stethoscope and reference cards, and leave the beach gear behind.

7. Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)

Definition: A simple rank-combination algorithm. For each document appearing in any ranked list, compute score = Σ 1/(k + rank) across all lists. Higher scores mean the document was ranked highly by more retrievers. No score calibration needed — works purely on ranks.

Simple Example: Document #42 is ranked 1st by BM25 and 5th by FAISS. With k=60: score = 1/(60+1) + 1/(60+5) = 0.0164 + 0.0154 = 0.0318. Document #99 is ranked 3rd by BM25 but absent from FAISS top-50: score = 1/(60+3) = 0.0159. Document #42 wins.

Analogy: Two movie critics rank their top 10 films. Instead of averaging their scores (which might be on different scales), you give points based purely on position: 1st place = 10 points, 2nd = 9, and so on. Films appearing in both lists get points from both critics. The combined ranking naturally favors films that both critics liked.

8. Dual-Temperature Sampling for Preference Pairs

Definition: A technique for generating DPO training pairs by sampling two answers from the same model at different temperatures. Low temperature (T=0.3) produces conservative, high-probability text. High temperature (T=0.9) produces diverse, exploratory text. A judge picks the better answer.

Simple Example: Question: "Dosage for amoxicillin in pediatric otitis media?" T=0.3 answer: safe boilerplate from the textbook. T=0.9 answer: might include creative details or might hallucinate a dosage. GPT-4o-mini picks the winner. This contrast creates the preference signal DPO needs.

Analogy: An improv comedy class exercise: each student performs the same scene twice — once "playing it safe" (T=0.3) and once "going wild" (T=0.9). The director watches both and picks which performance was better. Sometimes the safe version wins (accurate facts). Sometimes the wild version is brilliant (richer explanation). The student learns their own range.

Execute the Blueprint

Step 1: Data Preparation — Download, Deduplicate, Format

The first step is assembling the training corpus. We pull from two medalpaca datasets — 10,178 USMLE-style MCQ from medical_meadow_medqa and 10,000 open-ended Q&A from medical_meadow_wikidoc.

There is a subtle bug here. The medalpaca datasets store a constant template string in the instruction column ("Please answer with one of the option in the bracket") and the actual question in the input column. If you deduplicate on instruction alone, you get 2 unique rows instead of 18,000. The fix: concatenate instruction + input before deduplication. 

# scripts/prepare_sft_data.py

SOURCES = [
    ("medalpaca/medical_meadow_medqa", "instruction", "input", "output"),
    ("medalpaca/medical_meadow_wikidoc", "instruction", "input", "output"),
]

def load_all() -> Dataset:
    parts = []
    for repo, instr_col, input_col, resp_col in SOURCES:
        ds = load_dataset(repo, split="train")
        ds = ds.map(
            lambda ex: {
                "instruction": (
                    f"{ex[instr_col].strip()}\n\n{ex[input_col].strip()}"
                    if ex.get(input_col)
                    else ex[instr_col]
                ),
                "response": ex[resp_col],
            },
            remove_columns=ds.column_names,
        )
        parts.append(ds)
    return concatenate_datasets(parts)

The deduplication and ChatML formatting are handled by two compact functions:

# src/medalign/data/format.py

def to_chatml(instruction: str, response: str, system: str | None = None) -> str:
    sys = system or "You are a careful medical assistant. Be accurate and cite uncertainty."
    return (
        f"<|im_start|>system\n{sys}<|im_end|>\n"
        f"<|im_start|>user\n{instruction.strip()}<|im_end|>\n"
        f"<|im_start|>assistant\n{response.strip()}<|im_end|>"
    )

def dedup_minhash(texts: Iterable[str], threshold: float = 0.85) -> list[int]:
    """Return indices of texts to keep after near-duplicate removal."""
    lsh = MinHashLSH(threshold=threshold, num_perm=128)
    keep: list[int] = []
    for i, t in enumerate(texts):
        mh = _minhash(t)
        if not lsh.query(mh):
            lsh.insert(str(i), mh)
            keep.append(i)
    return keep

Every hyperparameter lives in a YAML config file. This makes iteration easy — you change a number in a text file, not in Python code.

Attention-Only LoRA
(4 modules, ~16M params)

  • Only adapts q_proj, k_proj, v_proj, o_proj
  • Misses MLP layers where domain knowledge lives
  • Lower memory cost (~16M trainable params)
  • Common in tutorials but leaves quality on the table

Full-Coverage LoRA
(7 modules, ~41M params)

  • Adapts all 7 projections including gate, up, down
  • MLP layers store drug names, dosage patterns
  • Slightly higher memory (~41M params, still <1%)
  • Measurably better medical accuracy

Step 3: SFT Training Entrypoint

The training entrypoint is a single function designed to be called from a Kaggle notebook. It handles the full sequence: load tokenizer, quantize model, inject LoRA, train, and push.

# src/medalign/training/sft.py

def run_sft(config_path: str, hub_repo: str | None = None) -> str:
    import torch
    # Force single-GPU: prevent Trainer from wrapping in DataParallel
    # (4-bit bnb tensors can't be replicated across devices)
    torch.cuda.device_count = lambda: 1

    cfg = yaml.safe_load(Path(config_path).read_text())
    m, l, t, d = cfg["model"], cfg["lora"], cfg["training"], cfg["data"]

    bnb = BitsAndBytesConfig(
        load_in_4bit=m["load_in_4bit"],
        bnb_4bit_quant_type=m["bnb_4bit_quant_type"],
        bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=getattr(torch, m["bnb_4bit_compute_dtype"]),
        bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True,
    )

    tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(m["base_model"], use_fast=True)
    if tokenizer.pad_token is None:
        tokenizer.pad_token = tokenizer.eos_token

    model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
        m["base_model"], quantization_config=bnb,
        device_map={"": 0}, torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    )
    model = prepare_model_for_kbit_training(model)

    peft_cfg = LoraConfig(
        r=l["r"], lora_alpha=l["alpha"], lora_dropout=l["dropout"],
        target_modules=l["target_modules"], bias="none", task_type="CAUSAL_LM",
    )
    model = get_peft_model(model, peft_cfg)
    model.print_trainable_parameters()

    dataset = load_dataset(d["dataset_id"], split="train")

    args = SFTConfig(
        output_dir=t["output_dir"], num_train_epochs=t["num_train_epochs"],
        per_device_train_batch_size=t["per_device_train_batch_size"],
        gradient_accumulation_steps=t["gradient_accumulation_steps"],
        learning_rate=t["learning_rate"], lr_scheduler_type=t["lr_scheduler_type"],
        warmup_ratio=t["warmup_ratio"], bf16=t["bf16"],
        logging_steps=t["logging_steps"], save_steps=t["save_steps"],
        optim=t["optim"], report_to="none",
        dataset_text_field="text", max_length=t["max_seq_length"], packing=False,
    )

    trainer = SFTTrainer(model=model, args=args, train_dataset=dataset,
                         processing_class=tokenizer)
    trainer.train()

    if hub_repo:
        model.push_to_hub(hub_repo, private=False)
        tokenizer.push_to_hub(hub_repo)
Three Critical Lines:
  • torch.cuda.device_count = lambda: 1 — Prevents DataParallel wrapping. 4-bit bnb tensors cannot be replicated across GPUs.
  • packing=False — One example per sequence. Loss computed on assistant tokens only, not the padding.
  • optim="paged_adamw_8bit" — Offloads optimizer states to CPU when GPU VRAM is tight.

Step 4: DPO Preference Pair Generation

After SFT, the model can imitate medical answers. But imitation is not alignment — it does not know which of two plausible answers is safer. To teach preference, we generate comparison pairs using dual-temperature sampling.

  1. Load the SFT model (base + adapters).
  2. For each of 2,000 medical prompts, generate two answers:
    • Answer A at T=0.3 (conservative, high-probability, textbook-safe)
    • Answer B at T=0.9 (creative, diverse, sometimes wrong)
  3. Send both to GPT-4o-mini: "You are a board-certified physician. Pick the more accurate, safe, and complete answer."
  4. Build DPO triples: (prompt, chosen, rejected).

Step 5: DPO Alignment Training

DPO is where the model learns preference, not just imitation. The training loop is deceptively simple — but three lines of code carry all the architectural weight.

# src/medalign/training/dpo.py

def run_dpo(config_path: str, hub_repo: str | None = None) -> str:
    import torch
    torch.cuda.device_count = lambda: 1

    cfg = yaml.safe_load(Path(config_path).read_text())
    m, dpo_c, t, d = cfg["model"], cfg["dpo"], cfg["training"], cfg["data"]

    bnb = BitsAndBytesConfig(
        load_in_4bit=True, bnb_4bit_quant_type="nf4",
        bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=torch.bfloat16, bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True,
    )

    tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(m["base_model"], use_fast=True)
    if tokenizer.pad_token is None:
        tokenizer.pad_token = tokenizer.eos_token

    base = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
        m["base_model"], quantization_config=bnb,
        device_map={"": 0}, torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    )
    # CRITICAL: is_trainable=True attaches SFT adapters as the trainable policy
    policy = PeftModel.from_pretrained(base, m["sft_adapter_repo"], is_trainable=True)

    dataset = load_dataset(d["dataset_id"], split="train")

    args = DPOConfig(
        output_dir=t["output_dir"], num_train_epochs=t["num_train_epochs"],
        per_device_train_batch_size=t["per_device_train_batch_size"],
        gradient_accumulation_steps=t["gradient_accumulation_steps"],
        learning_rate=t["learning_rate"],   # 5e-6: 40x LOWER than SFT!
        lr_scheduler_type=t["lr_scheduler_type"],
        warmup_ratio=t["warmup_ratio"],     # 0.1: longer warmup than SFT
        bf16=t["bf16"], logging_steps=t["logging_steps"],
        save_steps=t["save_steps"], optim=t["optim"],
        beta=dpo_c["beta"],                 # 0.1: KL pressure from DPO paper
        loss_type=dpo_c["loss_type"],       # sigmoid: original DPO
        max_length=dpo_c["max_length"],
        max_prompt_length=dpo_c["max_prompt_length"],
        report_to="none",
    )

    trainer = DPOTrainer(
        model=policy,
        ref_model=None,  # PEFT trick: TRL toggles adapters for reference
        args=args, train_dataset=dataset, processing_class=tokenizer,
    )
    trainer.train()

Naive DPO
(2 full model copies)

  • Load reference model: 14 GB (fp16)
  • Total VRAM: ~28 GB minimum
  • Won't fit on Kaggle T4 (16 GB)

PEFT-DPO Trick
(Adapter toggle, ref_model=None)

  • Load base in 4-bit: ~4 GB
  • Adapters on = policy (~40 MB extra)
  • Adapters off = reference (0 extra)
  • Saves 14 GB — fits on free T4
DPO is Fragile: If your learning rate is too high, the model collapses. If your preference pairs are bad, DPO learns the wrong preferences. Start with the paper defaults (β=0.1, lr=5e-6) and only change one thing at a time.

Step 6: Hybrid RAG Index + Retrieval

A fine-tuned model still hallucinates facts it never memorized. RAG grounds the model's answers in real medical literature. Our hybrid approach combines two retrieval paradigms to cover both semantic and keyword search.

# src/medalign/rag/hybrid.py

def reciprocal_rank_fusion(rankings: list[list[int]], k: int = 60) -> list[tuple[int, float]]:
    scores: dict[int, float] = {}
    for rank_list in rankings:
        for rank, doc_id in enumerate(rank_list):
            scores[doc_id] = scores.get(doc_id, 0.0) + 1.0 / (k + rank + 1)
    return sorted(scores.items(), key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)

class HybridRetriever:
    def search(self, query: str) -> list[dict]:
        r = self.cfg["retrieval"]
        # Stage 1: Dense retrieval (BGE-large embeddings + FAISS)
        q_emb = self.embedder.encode([query], normalize_embeddings=True)
        _, dense_ids = self.faiss.search(np.array(q_emb, dtype="float32"), r["top_k_dense"])
        dense_list = dense_ids[0].tolist()

        # Stage 2: Sparse retrieval (BM25 keyword matching)
        bm25_scores = self.bm25.get_scores(query.lower().split())
        sparse_list = np.argsort(bm25_scores)[::-1][: r["top_k_sparse"]].tolist()

        # Stage 3: RRF fusion (merge without score calibration)
        fused = reciprocal_rank_fusion([dense_list, sparse_list], k=r["rrf_k"])
        candidate_ids = [doc_id for doc_id, _ in fused[: max(20, r["final_top_k"] * 4)]]
        candidates = [self.meta[i] for i in candidate_ids]

        # Stage 4: Cross-encoder reranking (joint query+doc scoring)
        if self.reranker is not None:
            pairs = [(query, c["text"]) for c in candidates]
            scores = self.reranker.predict(pairs)
            order = np.argsort(scores)[::-1][: r["final_top_k"]]
            return [candidates[i] for i in order]
        return candidates[: r["final_top_k"]]

  • RRF merges without score calibration
  • "Metformin" found by BM25 even if BGE misses it
  • Cross-encoder reranker gives final precision boost

Step 7: Medical Calibration + GGUF Quantization

Before quantizing, we build a calibration corpus that tells the quantizer which weights matter most. Instead of generic Wikipedia text, we use 4,000 medical-domain chunks:

# scripts/build_calibration.py

def main(out_path: str = "data/calibration_medical.txt", n_samples: int = 2000):
    pubmed = load_dataset("qiaojin/PubMedQA", "pqa_artificial", split="train")
    medqa = load_dataset("medalpaca/medical_meadow_medqa", split="train")

    chunks = []
    for row in pubmed.select(range(min(n_samples, len(pubmed)))):
        ctx = row.get("context")
        if isinstance(ctx, dict):
            ctx = " ".join(ctx.get("contexts", []))
        if ctx:
            chunks.append(ctx)
    for row in medqa.select(range(min(n_samples, len(medqa)))):
        chunks.append(f"Q: {row['instruction']}\nA: {row['output']}")

    Path(out_path).write_text("\n\n".join(chunks))
    print(f"Wrote {len(chunks)} chunks -> {out_path}")

Step 8: Benchmarking

The whole point of a multi-stage pipeline is monotonic improvement. Each stage should score higher than the last on standardized benchmarks. We evaluate with three levels of rigor:

  1. Substring match — fast, deterministic, noisy (is the gold label literally in the output?)
  2. GPT-4o-mini judge — 4-dimension rubric scoring (accuracy, safety, completeness, citations)
  3. RAGAS metrics — faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision, context recall
# src/medalign/eval/llm_judge.py

JUDGE_SYSTEM = (
    "You are a board-certified physician evaluating an AI medical assistant. "
    "Score the answer from 1-5 on each rubric. Output ONLY valid JSON with keys: "
    "accuracy, safety, completeness, citations, rationale."
)

@dataclass
class JudgeScore:
    accuracy: float
    safety: float
    completeness: float
    citations: float | None
    rationale: str

def score_answer(question: str, reference: str, candidate: str,
                 model: str = "gpt-4o-mini") -> JudgeScore:
    client = OpenAI(api_key=os.environ["OPENAI_API_KEY"])
    resp = client.chat.completions.create(
        model=model,
        response_format={"type": "json_object"},
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": JUDGE_SYSTEM},
            {"role": "user", "content": JUDGE_USER.format(
                question=question, reference=reference, candidate=candidate)},
        ],
        temperature=0.0,
    )
    data = json.loads(resp.choices[0].message.content)
    return JudgeScore(
        accuracy=float(data["accuracy"]),
        safety=float(data["safety"]),
        completeness=float(data["completeness"]),
        citations=None if data.get("citations") in (None, "null")
                  else float(data["citations"]),
        rationale=str(data.get("rationale", "")),
    )

Step 9: Serving — FastAPI + Gradio

The serving architecture uses a shared answer_fn that powers both the REST API and the web UI. One bug fix, one improvement, one function.

# scripts/serve.py

def build_app(gguf_path: str, use_rag: bool):
    from fastapi import FastAPI
    from llama_cpp import Llama

    llm = Llama(model_path=gguf_path, n_ctx=4096, n_gpu_layers=0)
    retriever = None
    if use_rag:
        from medalign.rag.hybrid import HybridRetriever
        retriever = HybridRetriever(yaml.safe_load(Path("configs/rag.yaml").read_text()))

    app = FastAPI(title="MedAlign")

    def answer(question: str) -> dict:
        sources = []
        prompt = f"<|im_start|>user\n{question}<|im_end|>\n<|im_start|>assistant\n"
        if retriever is not None:
            docs = retriever.search(question)
            sources = [d["text"][:200] for d in docs]
            ctx = "\n".join(f"[{i+1}] {d['text']}" for i, d in enumerate(docs))
            prompt = (
                "<|im_start|>system\nAnswer using ONLY the context. Cite as [#].<|im_end|>\n"
                f"<|im_start|>user\nContext:\n{ctx}\n\nQuestion: {question}<|im_end|>\n"
                "<|im_start|>assistant\n"
            )
        out = llm(prompt, max_tokens=512, temperature=0.2, stop=["<|im_end|>"])
        return {"answer": out["choices"][0]["text"], "sources": sources}

    @app.post("/ask")
    def ask(q: Query):
        return answer(q.question)

    return app, answer

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

  1. The full post-training pipeline is reproducible on free hardware. Kaggle T4x2 for GPU stages, consumer laptop for CPU stages, HF Hub for artifact flow. Total cost: ~$5 (GPT-4o-mini API).
  2. QLoRA on ALL 7 projection layers matters. MLP layers (gate_proj, up_proj, down_proj) hold domain knowledge. Skipping them leaves measurable accuracy on the table.
  3. The PEFT-DPO trick makes alignment feasible on consumer GPUs. ref_model=None saves 14 GB by toggling adapters instead of maintaining two model copies.
  4. DPO is simpler than PPO but fragile. 40x lower learning rate than SFT. Bad preference pairs teach bad preferences. The GPT-4o-mini judge is the quality bottleneck.
  5. Hybrid RAG is worth the complexity. Dense catches semantics, sparse catches jargon, the cross-encoder reranker catches the rest. Medical queries need all three.
  6. Domain-specific imatrix preserves what matters during quantization. Generic calibration preserves "football" weights. Medical calibration preserves "metformin" weights.
  7. Shared answer_fn architecture prevents logic drift. FastAPI and Gradio calling the same function means one bug fix fixes both. Simple but critical.
